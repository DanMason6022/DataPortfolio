CortiLens: Smart Contact Lens for Cortisol Monitoring

About the Module

This project was part of NSCI3001: Science Enterprise, a capstone module offered to final-year students within Durham University‚Äôs Natural Sciences programme. The module is designed to simulate the commercialisation of scientific innovation, placing students in start-up teams to develop a hypothetical spin-out company.

The first few weeks consisted of targeted tutorials covering topics such as intellectual property, funding models, regulatory environments, ethical standards, and business planning in science-led industries. Students were then placed into teams and tasked with identifying an emerging area of science or technology to build a business around.

Alongside the group deliverables, we each produced an individual analysis report. This was a critical evaluation of our own team's business plan, exploring whether the proposal would genuinely hold up as a viable commercial venture. The assessment required not only analysing technical feasibility and financial projections, but also interrogating assumptions, identifying weaknesses, and proposing realistic improvements.

Science Enterprise offered a unique opportunity to blend scientific knowledge with entrepreneurial thinking, pushing us to go beyond lab-based understanding and engage with the real-world constraints of innovation, investment, and implementation.

Key Outcomes

The Business Plan and Presentation

Our project resulted in a comprehensive, multi-faceted business proposal that demonstrated both scientific innovation and commercial viability. Key deliverables and achievements included:

  • Full Business Plan: Authored a detailed 30+ page document outlining our product, market strategy, competitive landscape, regulatory pathway, intellectual property position, and financial forecasts.
  • Strategic Investor Pitch: Designed and delivered a concise, high-impact presentation summarising our value proposition to an academic panel simulating potential investors.
  • Innovative Product Concept: Proposed a non-invasive, continuously monitoring cortisol-sensing smart contact lens, supported by research into enzyme-based electrochemical detection.
  • Market Validation: Conducted primary market research through public surveys and competitive analysis, confirming interest, pricing feasibility, and strong support for a wearable cortisol tracker ‚Äî particularly among younger users and those with chronic stress or rare diseases.
  • Funding Strategy: Built a hypothetical but grounded financial model incorporating grant income, angel investment, and strategic partnerships ‚Äî projecting potential NHS revenue streams and subscription-based expansion into the wellness market.
  • Cross-functional Collaboration: Coordinated closely with team members across scientific, legal, financial, and operations domains to ensure that messaging and planning were cohesive and credible.

The Critical Appraisal

  • Scientific Foundation: The proposed use of the 11Œ≤-HSD2 enzyme for tear-based cortisol monitoring is theoretically plausible and well-researched. However, technical elements like RF antenna heating and PET lens discomfort were identified as needing further development and validation during R&D.
  • Market Risks: While targeting rare diseases aligns with a strong ethical mission, the narrow market (less than 14 people per 100,000 for Addison‚Äôs and fewer for Cushing‚Äôs) presents significant financial risk. I recommended treating this niche as a proof-of-concept phase, with later expansion into the stress management sector for sustainability.
  • Underestimated Costs: The original business plan significantly underestimated staff salaries, especially for key roles like CFO, CLO, and research scientists. I highlighted that realistic salary adjustments could increase projected ineligible costs by over ¬£500,000, posing a challenge to cash flow and grant eligibility.
  • Pricing Strategy: I argued that CortiLens‚Äô proposed pricing structure ‚Äî selling each lens to the NHS with just 55p of profit ‚Äî was economically unviable. A modest price increase would still yield cost savings for the NHS while making the venture more attractive to investors and founders alike.
  • Long-Term Viability: I concluded that the business plan, in its current state, would not be commercially feasible. However, with clearer technical validation, more grounded cost planning, and a realistic expansion pathway, CortiLens could evolve into a sustainable and scalable health tech company.

This critical assessment allowed me to bridge technical and strategic thinking ‚Äî applying financial logic, industry benchmarking, and scientific evaluation to challenge assumptions and propose meaningful improvements.

Together, these outcomes demonstrated that CortiLens could be more than a science fiction idea ‚Äî it had the potential to address a genuine unmet clinical need while fitting into real-world healthcare systems and consumer behaviour.
